Entries for Markers

Uncover historical markers that commemorate Jose Rizal’s life and journey, etched into cities across the globe.

SINGAPORE


The José Rizal marker in Singapore is located near the Asian Civilisations Museum by Anderson Bridge. It honors Rizal’s visits to the island in 1882, 1887, 1891, and 1896. The two-sided marker features a painting by Fabián de la Rosa and a bronze relief by Guillermo Tolentino, along with Rizal’s quote: “There can be no tyrants where there are no slaves.” (Backpacking Philippines: Jose Rizal in Singapore ( a Walking Tour), n.d.)








HONGKONG


From December 1891 to June 1892, Dr. José Rizal, the national hero of the Philippines, resided in this area with his family. They lived at Rednaxela Terrace. During this time, Rizal also opened a medical clinic where he practiced as an ophthalmic surgeon. The marker says “PHILIPPINE PHYSICIAN, POET, AUTHOR AND PATRIOT PRACTICED MEDICINE IN 1891-1892 IN A CLINIC WHICH ONCE STOOD ON THIS SITE” (Chasing the Patriots Down the Filipinas Heritage Trail in Hong Kong – Tulay 橋, 2021)






SCHAFFHAUSEN, SWITZERLAND


To commemorate Rizal’s 100th birth anniversary, In December 1961, a memorial plaque was installed on the façade of the Hotel Muller by the Philippine ambassador to Switzerland at the time, Tomas Castro. The marker reads: “Jose Rizal (1861-1896) National Hero of the Philippines. Rizal, with Dr. Maximo Viola, stopped at Hotel Muller, 2-3 June, 1887.” his friend Maximo Viola, who had generously loaned Rizal money in order to get Noli Me Tangere published, arrived by train in Schaffhausen, Switzerland, on June 2, 1887. They stayed at Hotel Müller on Bahnhofstrasse 28. While the reason for visiting this small Swiss town is unclear, it was said that they were likely attracted to the Rhine Falls, Europe’s most powerful waterfall. According to Fadul, the two did visit the falls during their stay (Caña, 2020).






HEIDELBERG, GERMANY


An apartment fronting the University of Heidelberg, remains a well-preserved bronze marker that has been erected at the façade of this structure. A plaque that commemorates not only his training days at the Augen Clinic, the university eye clinic but he wrote the poem, “A los Flores de Heidelberg” here during his stay. Dr. Otto Becker, a leading ophthalmologist and professor at the University of Heidelberg, mentored Rizal, who was among his favored students. Rizal had access to the newly developed ophthalmoscope during his training. He lived in an apartment across from the university, while the eye clinic was situated near Bismarckplatz, away from the main university campus (Flores, 2011).






WILMHELSFELD, GERMANY


A plaque with gold letters was installed in the 100-year-old home of the German Pastor Ullmer who once hosted the stay of our national hero at Wilmhelsfeld in 1886. This house is where Rizal completed the final chapters of his novel “Noli Me Tangere”. In recognition of his legacy, the street in front of the house was named in his honor (Mawis, 2019).






VIENNA, AUSTRIA


A commemorative plaque honoring Dr. José Rizal was installed at Leopold Hof in Vienna on June 19, 1995, by Philippine Ambassador Reynaldo Arcilla, marking Rizal’s 134th birth anniversary. The plaque that translates: "DR. JOSE P. RIZAL, FILIPINO NATIONAL HERO, RESIDED FROM THE MAY 20 to 24, 1887 IN THE HOTEL METROPOLE WHICH WAS IN THIS PLACE. AN AUSTRIAN TEACHER, PROFESSOR FERDINAND BLUMENTRITT SUPPORTED RIZAL'S ACTION IN EUROPE AGAINST SPANISH COLONIAL RULE." Ferdinand Blumentritt, Austrian scholar and A close friend of Rizal whom he met at the Leitmeritz (a town in the Ust nad Labem Region of the Czech Republic) railroad station and became Rizal’s correspondents during his travels in Europe (Vienna 101, 2025).






SAN FRANCISCO, USA


A commemorative plaque on the side of the Palace Hotel in downtown San Francisco marks Dr. Jose P. Rizal’s brief stay there from May 4 to 6, 1888, his only visit to the United States. The maker also stated how he has widely been regarded as the leader for the Philippines freedom movement because of his writings like “Noli Me Tangere” and “El Filibusterismo”, which inspired the revolution. Originally a law student, Rizal shifted to medicine after learning that his mother was going blind. He later became an ophthalmologist and successfully performed the surgery to restore her vision (Edelman, n.d.).
